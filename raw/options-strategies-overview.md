# Options Strategies — Overview

An **options strategy** is a structured plan for trading options contracts to
achieve a specific financial goal — generating income, hedging risk, or
speculating on price moves — based on your outlook for the underlying asset.[^1]

## Core objectives and market outlook

Options strategies are grouped by **objective** and the trader's **market
outlook**:[^2]

| Objective | Typical outlook | Example strategies |
|-----------|-----------------|--------------------|
| **Income generation** | Neutral to bullish | Covered calls, cash-secured puts |
| **Hedging / protection** | Neutral to bearish | Protective puts, collars |
| **Speculation** | Directional or volatile | Long straddles, vertical spreads, strangles |

## Income-focused strategies

### Covered call
You own the stock and sell (write) a call option against it.
- **Benefit**: Earn premium income; profitable even if the stock barely moves.
- **Risk**: Upside capped at the strike price plus premium; you still bear the
  stock's downside.[^2]

### Cash-secured put
You sell a put option while keeping enough cash to buy the stock if assigned.
- **Benefit**: Earn premium; potentially acquire the stock at a lower, targeted
  price.
- **Risk**: If the stock falls sharply you must buy above market; profit is
  limited to the premium if the stock rises.[^2]

## Hedging / protection strategies

### Protective put
You own the stock and buy a put option as "insurance."
- **Benefit**: Limits downside; you can sell at the strike even in a crash.
- **Risk**: You pay the premium, which decays over time; puts can expire
  worthless.[^2]

### Collar
You own the stock, buy a protective put, and sell a covered call to finance the
put.
- **Benefit**: Defines both downside protection and an upside cap, often at low
  net cost.
- **Risk**: Upside limited by the short call; early-assignment risk on the
  call.[^2]

## Speculative / directional strategies

### Long straddle
Buy a call and a put with the same strike and expiration.
- **Benefit**: Profits from large moves in either direction; risk limited to
  total premium paid.
- **Risk**: Needs a big move to overcome the cost of both options; time decay
  hurts if the market stays flat.[^2]

### Vertical spreads
(bull call spread, bear put spread, etc.) Buy and sell options of the same type
(calls or puts), same expiration, different strikes.
- **Benefit**: Defined risk and reward; lower capital outlay than buying stock.
- **Risk**: Profit capped; short leg can be assigned early.[^2]

## How to choose a strategy

Your choice depends on:

- **Market view**: bullish, bearish, neutral, or expecting high/low volatility.
- **Risk tolerance**: how much loss you can accept and whether you want defined
  risk.
- **Capital and margin**: some strategies (e.g. spreads) require margin accounts.
- **Time horizon**: short-term trades vs. longer-dated positions.
- **Costs**: multi-leg strategies incur more commissions and fees.[^3]

## Getting started safely

- Study the basics of calls, puts, strike prices, expiration, and **the Greeks**
  (delta, gamma, theta, vega).
- Paper-trade or start small to understand how options behave.
- Read your broker's options disclosure (e.g. the OCC's *Characteristics and
  Risks of Standardized Options*).[^2]
